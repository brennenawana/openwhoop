# Ditch the tautology: use within-night percentiles for Deep, redefine "resting HR" separately

### Recommended approach

**Option 4 — a decoupled two-part fix.** (a) For the **Deep-sleep rule**, replace the `hr_mean < baseline.resting_hr + 8` gate with a **within-night percentile condition** (Option 3's spirit, with safeguards), matching the normalization convention the rule already uses for `hf_power` and `rmssd`. (b) Separately, **redefine `baseline.resting_hr`** as an awake-resting proxy (e.g., the upper-tail of sleep-epoch HR, or an explicit pre-sleep quiet-wake window), because that metric has a second job — feeding strain and sleep-need calculations — where absolute-physiology semantics matter. The literature is unanimous that modern wrist/PPG sleep classifiers normalize HR **per night** (Walch 2019; Roberts 2020; Altini & Kinnunen 2021; Perez-Pozuelo et al. 2022), that **HR alone is a weak discriminator for N3 vs N1/N2** so the HR gate should be a soft contributor rather than a hard threshold (Shaffer & Ginsberg 2017; Tobaldini et al. 2013; Sridhar et al. 2020), and that **WHOOP itself already derives its user-facing "resting HR" from the final SWS episode**, which is precisely the tautology the engineer ran into (WHOOP product docs; Bellenger et al. 2021). Options 1 (widen offset) and 2-alone (redefine RHR only) either mask the pathology or leak the fix into unrelated metrics; Option 3-alone leaves the degenerate-zero-Deep-night concern unaddressed. Option 4 cleanly separates the concerns and aligns with published practice.

### Key evidence

- **WHOOP's own "baseline resting HR" is computed during sleep, weighted toward the final slow-wave-sleep episode** (whoop.com product docs; Emily Capodilupo, WHOOP Podcast #29). This is exactly why using it as the reference for Deep detection is tautological on a WHOOP strap — the denominator *is* the quantity you're trying to flag.
- **Altini & Kinnunen (Oura, 2021; Sensors 21:4302, DOI 10.3390/s21134302)** apply **per-night robust normalization using the 5th–95th percentiles** to every feature before classification, and in their ablation HRV adds ~16% accuracy over motion-only while adding raw mean HR does relatively little — their Deep-sleep discrimination rides on HF-power/RMSSD after per-night scaling, not on absolute BPM.
- **Walch et al. (Apple Watch, 2019; Sleep 42:zsz180, DOI 10.1093/sleep/zsz180)** normalize HR per night by **dividing by the 90th percentile of |HR − mean_HR_night|** after a difference-of-Gaussians filter (σ₁=120 s, σ₂=600 s). Quote from Methods: *"Each individual was normalized by dividing by the 90th percentile in the absolute difference between each heart rate measurement and the mean heart rate over the sleep period."* Code: github.com/ojwalch/sleep_classifiers.
- **Roberts et al. (2020; Sleep 43:zsaa045, DOI 10.1093/sleep/zsaa045)** tested raw vs night-level z-score and found night-level z-scoring consistently improved performance. Quote: *"night-level normalization refers to transforming each feature within each night … to a z-score via subtracting the mean and dividing by the SD of that feature within the night."*
- **Perez-Pozuelo / HypnosPy (2022; Sci Rep 12:7956, DOI 10.1038/s41598-022-11792-7)** is the single clearest published precedent for the engineer's **Option 3** formulation — a **rule-based, empirical-CDF (ECDF) quantile gate on HR**. Quote: *"assigning binary wake/sleep labels whenever the participant's heart rate dips above/below a specific quantile threshold (Q) … calculated from the ECDF."* Grid-searched Q ≈ 0.325 of the 24-h distribution (free-living) or Q ≈ 0.80 within the night-only window, with a 10-min rolling-SD refinement at 6 bpm. This is the published existence proof that within-night percentile HR is a defensible rule-based gate.
- **Xiao et al. (2013; Biomed Signal Process Control 8:624–633)** explicitly introduces a *"fractile-values based method"* — per-subject percentile-rank normalization of HRV features — to handle between-subject variance. Direct ancestor of Option 3.
- **Sridhar et al. (2020; npj Digit Med 3:106, DOI 10.1038/s41746-020-0291-x)** within-night z-scores the instantaneous HR series before deep-learning staging, and reaches 4-class κ = 0.66 on SHHS from HR alone — but only because the NN extracts HRV dynamics from the beat series, not from mean BPM.
- **Task Force of the European Society of Cardiology and NASPE (1996; Circulation 93:1043–1065)** is the canonical HRV standard: HF (0.15–0.40 Hz) reflects vagal tone. **Tobaldini et al. (2013; Front Physiol 4:294)** reviews that N3 is characterized by peak HF / RMSSD and minimum LF/HF — parasympathetic dominance, not simply minimum HR. **Shaffer & Ginsberg (2017; Front Public Health 5:258, DOI 10.3389/fpubh.2017.00258)** confirms RMSSD and HF as the short-term vagal markers.
- **Trinder et al. (2001; J Sleep Res 10:253–264)** — the wake-to-N3 HR drop in healthy young adults is typically **5–10 bpm**, with the nadir in late-night SWS.
- **Bellenger et al. (2021; Sensors 21:3571)** reports WHOOP's HR/HRV is specifically sampled during SWS episodes; bias vs ECG ≈ 0.1 bpm during PSG-defined SWS.
- **Miller et al. (2020; J Sports Sci 38:2631–2636, DOI 10.1080/02640414.2020.1797448)** — WHOOP 4-stage κ = 0.47, SWS sensitivity 68% in young healthy adults. **Miller et al. (2021; Biosensors 11:185)** — WHOOP-AUTO underestimates SWS by −15.5 min on average. Neither paper discloses algorithm internals.
- **Schyvens et al. (2025; SLEEP Advances 6:zpaf021)** is currently the best independent WHOOP 4.0 head-to-head vs PSG (N=62, mean age 46).

### Per-question answers

- **Q1 — How is the HR reference point typically defined in published wrist/PPG Deep-sleep classifiers?** The dominant convention in modern ML pipelines is **within-night normalization**: per-night z-score (Roberts 2020; Sridhar 2020), per-night 90th-percentile dispersion scaling (Walch 2019), per-night 5th–95th-percentile robust scaling (Altini & Kinnunen 2021), or per-subject fractile-rank normalization (Xiao 2013). **Awake-measured resting HR as an explicit feature is essentially not used** in peer-reviewed wrist-staging algorithms — in part because consumer devices (Fitbit, Oura, WHOOP) *themselves derive user-facing "RHR" from sleep*, conflating the two (Quer et al. 2020, PLOS ONE 15:e0227709; WHOOP docs). Nightly minimum HR is a user-facing output, not a classifier input. No published wrist-based Deep classifier I can find uses "mean of 14-night nightly-minimum HR" as a reference threshold — the engineer's scheme is unusual.

- **Q2 — Normative gap between nightly minimum HR and awake resting HR.** In healthy young adults the SWS-trough HR is roughly **5–10 bpm below relaxed-wake HR** (Trinder et al. 2001, J Sleep Res 10:253–264; de Zambotti et al. 2018, Neurosci Biobehav Rev 90:84–103). Clinical summaries quote a 20–30% sleep-vs-daytime differential, but that compares daytime-active HR to sleep HR, not supine-quiet-wake to SWS. For endurance-trained adults, awake RHR of 40–55 bpm with nocturnal minima in the 30s is routine (Reimers et al. 2018; Nuuttila et al. 2022, PLOS ONE 17:e0262333), and the absolute floor is lower but the dip itself is still ~5–10 bpm. **The engineer's athletic user (awake 58–62, nightly min 48) sits in a realistic 10–14 bpm gap — larger than typical but plausible.** Critically, because WHOOP's own published-to-user RHR is weighted toward the final SWS episode, the *literal* gap between WHOOP's "resting HR" and the nightly minimum will be smaller than physiology-textbook numbers suggest, further collapsing the headroom available for an `+8 bpm` offset rule.

- **Q3 — How discriminating is HR alone for Deep vs Light in wrist staging?** **Weak.** Mean HR differs by only 2–5 bpm between N2 and N3 in most subjects, with large within-subject overlap; what actually distinguishes N3 is **parasympathetic dominance expressed through HF power and RMSSD**, plus **motion quiescence** (Tobaldini et al. 2013; Shaffer & Ginsberg 2017; ESC/NASPE Task Force 1996). Feature-importance ablations are consistent: Walch (2019) notes HR and circadian features beat motion for three-class staging; Altini & Kinnunen (2021) find HRV adds ~16% accuracy on top of motion and dominates N3 discrimination; Sridhar et al. (2020) can reach 4-class κ ≈ 0.66 from HR-derived signal **only because the neural net extracts HRV-like dynamics from the beat series** — not from mean BPM. Beattie et al. (2017, Physiol Meas 38:1968) confirm combined motion+HRV ≈ doubles κ over either alone. Implication for the Rust classifier: **the `hr_mean` condition is doing a small fraction of the Deep-detection work; `hf_power` and `rmssd` gates are the real workhorses.** That is also why the current rule's over-strict HR gate is so damaging — it's vetoing epochs that the stronger features correctly flag as Deep.

- **Q4 — Is there any published approach that uses within-night percentile of HR as a Deep gate?** **Yes, for sleep/wake; not explicitly for Deep.** Perez-Pozuelo et al. (2022, Sci Rep 12:7956; HypnosPy) is the clearest precedent, using an ECDF quantile threshold (Q≈0.325 free-living, 0.80 night-only) to gate sleep vs wake, with a 10-min rolling-SD-of-HR refinement. Walch (2019) uses a 90th-percentile dispersion denominator; Altini & Kinnunen (2021) use the 5th/95th percentiles for per-night robust scaling. The exact formulation "HR below the 25th percentile of the night → Deep candidate" has not been published as a rule-based gate — but it's a straight-line extension of the same family of conventions, and it matches the philosophy the engineer's rule already applies to HF power ("> 50th percentile of this night's HF"). **For forward-compatibility with ML, it's also the form most compatible with Phase-2 LightGBM training.**

- **Q5 — Validation-study feature sets and normalization.**
  - **Wulterkens et al. (2021, Nat Sci Sleep)** used a 132-HRV-feature bank (time-, frequency-, non-linear, detrended-fluctuation, Teager) fed into an LSTM trained on Philips wearable vs PSG; HRV features carry the discrimination, with per-subject input normalization inside the LSTM training loop — no absolute BPM thresholds.
  - **Fonseca et al. (2020, Sleep 43:zsaa048) / Fonseca et al. (2017)** — ECG-trained multi-class Bayesian LDA, validated on wrist PPG; raw features with ML absorbing between-subject HR variance; 4-class κ ≈ 0.42 (2017), improved by LSTM in 2020.
  - **Altini & Kinnunen (Oura, 2021; Sensors 21:4302)** — motion (trimmed-mean/max/IQR per axis, arm-angle delta), PPG HR and HRV, skin-temperature, and a **sensor-independent circadian factor** (cosine drive + homeostatic pressure + time-since-sleep-onset). **Per-night 5th–95th-percentile robust normalization** of every feature before LightGBM. **No absolute HR threshold anywhere.**
  - **Beattie et al. (2017, Physiol Meas 38:1968)** — 180 candidate features (motion, PPG-derived HRV time-domain, PPG-baseline respiration) pruned to 54; LDA classifier; 4-class κ ≈ 0.52. Features include inter-beat-interval statistics and local HR deviations from a trailing-window mean — **relative, not absolute**, though the paper does not apply explicit night-level z-scoring.
  - **Kotzen et al. ("SleepPPG-Net", 2022; arXiv 2202.05735 / IEEE JBHI)** — deep-learning directly on raw PPG at 34.13 Hz; standard waveform preprocessing and normalization inside the network; no hand-crafted HR thresholds at all.
  - **Common thread:** none of these five use absolute BPM thresholds for Deep; every one either normalizes per-subject/per-night or lets an ML model absorb the individual variance.
  
  For the **MESA / bzhai pipeline** specifically (Zhai et al. 2020, IMWUT 4:67, DOI 10.1145/3397325; github.com/bzhai/multimodal_sleep_stage_benchmark): RR outlier filter (300–2000 ms, Tanaka et al.) → NN interpolation → 30-s epoch alignment with actigraphy → full HRV feature bank → **a single global z-score across the pooled training set** (scikit-learn `StandardScaler`), not per-subject and not per-night. This matters for Phase 2: if the engineer feature-engineers within-night-normalized HR on-device, those features will be distributionally comparable to the MESA pipeline's z-scored features, because *both* collapse between-subject BPM differences — but the engineer should explicitly **document that the on-device features are per-night-z-scored** and re-fit the LightGBM model on features normalized the same way, not on bzhai's pooled-z-score features directly.

- **Q6 — Athletic-population considerations.** No paper isolates "staging accuracy as a function of RHR," but three convergent lines of evidence indicate the problem is real: (i) Sargent et al. (2016, J Sci Med Sport 19:848–853) and Kawasaki et al. (2022, Nat Sci Sleep 14:819–827; Fitbit Alta HR in college athletes, N3 r=0.68) both show degraded stage-specific accuracy in athletes vs general population; (ii) all major consumer validations show **poor wake specificity (~50%)** — the problem is quiet wake getting scored as sleep, and in athletes whose awake-bed HR overlaps population deep-sleep HR, this confusion is mechanically worse; (iii) Miller et al. (2020, 2021) show WHOOP in particular underestimates SWS by ~15 min in young adults. The engineer's specific failure mode — `mean(nightly_min)` as the reference producing a 56-bpm Deep threshold for a 48-bpm-min athlete — is a textbook example of the athletic miscalibration the literature warns about structurally, even if no paper names it explicitly.

### If I chose differently

**Picking Option 1 (widen the offset to +12 or +15 bpm)** papers over the pathology without fixing it. It will likely restore Deep% to plausible range on this one athletic subject, but it inflates N3 in average users (the offset is too permissive when `resting_hr` is already near the nightly minimum), and it does nothing to address the underlying fact that `baseline.resting_hr` is a Deep-derived quantity. Expect per-subject tuning to creep in; expect over-detection in heavy-dipper non-athletes.

**Picking Option 2 alone (redefine `resting_hr` as 25th percentile of sleep HR or mean of non-Wake epochs)** fixes the Deep rule but tangles the strain and sleep-need calculations, which want a stable absolute anchor. The 25th-percentile-of-sleep-HR is still Deep-adjacent (N3 typically falls in the lower quartile of nightly HR, per Trinder 2001) so the tautology is only partially broken. Mean-of-non-Wake is better but requires a reliable Wake detector upstream, which on WHOOP data is itself noisy (per ~50% wake specificity ceiling across consumer devices).

**Picking Option 3 alone (within-night percentile, e.g., HR < 25th percentile)** is the closest to published practice and forward-compatible with the Phase-2 LightGBM model, but has two watchouts: (i) on nights with zero true Deep, the 25th percentile still mechanically flags ~25% of epochs as Deep-candidates — mitigate by keeping the conjunction with the already-in-place `hf_power > 50th percentile` and `rmssd > baseline` and `motion_stillness > 0.95` gates, which jointly are rarely satisfied on a true Deep-free night; (ii) the absolute-physiology semantics (reporting "sleeping HR = 50 bpm") disappear from the Deep rule, but those semantics belong to the user-facing `resting_hr` field, not the Deep gate. Option 4's dual fix keeps the user-facing semantics while letting the classifier run on relative features.

### Uncertainties / what the literature can't tell me

No peer-reviewed paper publishes WHOOP's sleep-staging algorithm internals — every WHOOP validation (Miller 2020, 2021; Bellenger 2021; Berryhill 2020; Schyvens 2024, 2025) treats it as a black box, so we can't anchor the Rust classifier's thresholds to WHOOP's own conventions except via WHOOP's public product documentation (which defines "resting HR" as weighted toward the last SWS episode). No published rule-based consumer Deep-sleep classifier uses an explicit within-night HR percentile *gate* (Perez-Pozuelo 2022 is the closest, but it's a sleep/wake gate, not a Deep gate), so Option 3 and the HR half of Option 4 have strong *principled* support from the ML pipelines (Walch, Roberts, Altini, Xiao, Sridhar) but no direct rule-based precedent — the engineer is extending a published ML convention into rule form. The literature is also silent on the exact staging-accuracy penalty as a function of resting HR: athletic-population validations (Sargent 2016; Kawasaki 2022; Miller 2020) document degraded accuracy without isolating RHR as the covariate. Finally, the quantile to choose for a Deep gate (20th? 25th? 15th?) is not empirically calibrated in any paper — Perez-Pozuelo's Q=0.80 for night-only sleep/wake implies a much higher quantile than you'd want for Deep, and the right Deep quantile depends on the user's true Deep fraction (10–30% of TST, but trending down with age per AASM norms), so this will need on-device tuning against the target Deep% range until Phase-2 MESA-trained validation is available.
